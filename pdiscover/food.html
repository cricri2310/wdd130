Peruvian Cuisine

Peruvian cuisine is like a boom of mixed flavors and traditions that have been influenced by a lot of different cultures over the years. Originally, Peru's ancient civilizations, such as the Incas, based their food on local ingredients such as potatoes, corn and quinoa. In fact, potatoes come from Peru, where there are more than 3,000 different types. They also ate fish from the coast and guinea pig, which was one of the main sources of protein in the Andes.When the Spanish arrived, they brought their own ingredients and culinary techniques, which completely changed Peruvian food. The Spaniards introduced beef, chicken and citrus fruits, and also brought African slaves, who brought their own flavors and recipes into the mix. This blend of Spanish, African and native traditions gave rise to a whole new style of cooking, in which ingredients were combined in novel ways, creating dishes such as anticuchos (grilled meat skewers) and aji de gallina (a creamy, spicy chicken dish).Later, immigrants from China and Japan brought their own influence to Peruvian gastronomy. The Chinese introduced stir-fry, which they mixed with local ingredients to create chifa, a Chinese-Peruvian fusion cuisine popular in Peru today. Japanese immigrants inspired dishes such as ceviche, the famous Peruvian dish of raw fish marinated in lime juice, which has both Japanese and Peruvian roots. All these influences make Peruvian food one of the most diverse and richest cuisines in the world, with traditional recipes that have grown and changed over the centuries to become totally unique.
